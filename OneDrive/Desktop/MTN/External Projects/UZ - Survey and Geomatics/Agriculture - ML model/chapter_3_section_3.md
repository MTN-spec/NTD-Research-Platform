# 3.3 Study Area Description

This section describes the geographical and agricultural characteristics of the study area. The research was conducted across a spatial extent that spans both the Makonde and Chinhoyi districts in Mashonaland West. These two administrative areas are separated by a boundary that runs through the primary farming zones near Lion's Den. A major feature of this landscape is the Angwa Dam which provides a critical source of water for the surrounding fields. The study area sits at an approximate elevation of 1100 metres above the sea level. This region falls within the Natural Region IIa agro-ecological zone of Zimbabwe. Mugandani et al. (2021) observed that the area benefits from reliable rainfall patterns during the summer months. Precipitation typically ranges between 750 and 1000 millimetres annually from November to April.

The local soil profile is dominated by deep red clay loams. Nyamapfeni (1991) classified these as orthoferrallitic soils which are known for their high moisture retention capacity. Such soil properties support intensive maize production using center pivot irrigation technology. Maize represents the main crop in this district for national food security. The growing season begins with the first rains in late November. Peak moisture demand for the crop occurs during the flowering stage in February. Farmers in these districts increasingly utilize supplemental irrigation from the Angwa Dam to mitigate mid-season dry spells.

The selection of this dual-district site is justified by its economic importance. It represents a vital section of the Zimbabwean breadbasket. The study area includes a variety of center pivot farms that are strategically distributed across the district boundary. This distribution allows for a robust assessment of the soil moisture alert system in diverse operational environments. Access to these georeferenced fields enabled the collection of accurate ground-truth data for model validation.
